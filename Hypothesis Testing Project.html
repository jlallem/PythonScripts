Define Problem Statement and Objectives¶

Problem Statement¶

Business Context¶

E-news Express is an online news source that executives want to grow through gaining new subscribers. The company plans to analyze actions taken by each visitor to better understand user interests; one of the main goals is to determine what drives better engagement. Executives at E-news Express believe there has been a drop in new monthly subscribers due to the poor quality of the existing webpage. Specifically, the executives are concerned about the outline and recommended content needed to keep customers engaged long enough for them to subscribe.

Objective¶

The E-news Express design team has created a new landing page. This new landing page has a new outline and more relevant content relative to the current page. To test how effective the new landing page is at gaining new subscribers, the Data Science team randomly selected 100 users that were divided equally into two groups. The first (control) group observed the existing landing page while the second (treatment) group observed the new landing page. Data on the interaction of the users with their respective landing pages was collected. As a data scientist, you have been asked to explore the data and perform statistical analysis - using a 5% level of significance - to determine how effective the new landing page is at gaining new subscribers. To do this analysis, the following questions must be addressed:

  • Do the users spend more time on the new landing page than on the old landing page?
  • Is the conversion rate for the new page greater than the conversion rate for the old page?
  • How does the user's preferred language relate to converted status?
  • Is the time spent on the new page the same for users with different preferred languages?

Data Dictionary¶

The data contains information regarding the interaction of users in both groups with the two versions of the landing page.

  • user_id - Unique user ID of the person visiting the website
  • group - Whether the user belongs to the first group (control) or the second group (treatment)
  • landing_page - Whether the landing page is new or old
  • time_spent_on_the_page - Time (in minutes) spent by the user on the landing page
  • converted - Whether the user gets converted to a subscriber of the news portal or not
  • language_preferred - Language chosen by the user to view the landing page

Step 1: Define the null and alternate hypotheses¶

Let $\mu_1$ be the mean time spent on the new landing page and $\mu_2$ be the mean time spent on the old landing page.

The null hypothesis is that the mean time spent on the new landing page is equal to the mean time spent on the old landing page.

$H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2$

The alternative hypothesis is that the mean time spent on the new landing page is greater than the mean time spent on the old landing page.

$H_a:\mu_1>\mu_2$

Are the T-test assumptions are satisfied or not?¶

  • Continuous data - Yes, the time_spent_on_the_page is on a continuous scale.
  • Normally distributed populations - Yes, time_spent_on_the_page appears to be normally distributed.
  • Independent populations - Yes, as we are taking random samples for two different landing_page types, the two samples are from two independent populations. Additionally, it is given that the sample observations are independent of each other.
  • Unequal population standard deviations - Yes, as the sample standard deviations are different, the population standard deviations cannot be assumed to be equal. Population standard deviations are unknown.
  • Random sampling from the population - Yes, the collected sample is a simple random sample.

We can use two sample independent t-test (unequal standard deviations) for this problem.

Are the Z-test assumptions satisfied or not?¶

  • Binomially distributed population - Yes, users are either converted to subscribers or not.
  • Random sampling from the population - Yes, we are informed that the sample is a random sample that is evenly split into two groups.
  • Can the binomial distribution be approximated to a normal distribution - Yes, CLT works for binary data, but must be tested. We must check whether np and n(1-p) are greater than or equal to 10. *In this case, n is sample size and p is sample proportion.

$np_1 = 50\cdot \frac{33}{50} =33 \geq 10\\ n(1-p_1) = 50 \cdot \frac{50-33}{50} =17 \geq 10 \\ np_2 = 50\cdot \frac{21}{50} =21 \geq 10\\ n(1-p_2) = 50 \cdot \frac{50-21}{50} =29 \geq 10 $

Let $\mu_1, \mu_2, \mu_3$ be the means of the time spent on the new page for preferred language equal to Spanish, French, and English, respectively.

We will test the null hypothesis:

$H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \mu_3$

Are the assumptions satisified for a One-Way ANOVA F-Test?¶

  • The populations are normally distributed - Yes, based on the Shapiro-Wilk's test.
  • The samples are independent random samples - Yes, we are informed that this is true.
  • Population variances are equal - Yes, based on Levene's test.

We can use a One-Way ANOVA F-Test to find the p-value to test the Null Hypothesis.

test_stat, p_value = f_oneway(df_new.loc[df_new['language_preferred'] == 'Spanish', 'time_spent_on_the_page'],
                              df_new.loc[df_new['language_preferred'] == 'French', 'time_spent_on_the_page'],
                              df_new.loc[df_new['language_preferred'] == 'English', 'time_spent_on_the_page'])
print('The p-value is ' + str(round(p_value,5)))

The p-value is 0.43204

Observations¶

The p-value is much larger at 0.43204 than the significance level of 0.05, therefore we do not have sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis that the time spent on the new page is the same for any of the preferred languages.

Conclusion and Business Recommendations¶

Conclusions¶

  • Overall mean time spent on the landing page is 5.38 minutes.
    • Overall, first quartile, median, and third quartile are 3.88 minutes, 5.42 minutes, and 7.02 minutes, respectively for time spent on the landing page.
    • Mean time spent on the new landing page is 6.22 minutes, while the mean time spent on the old landing page is 4.53 minutes.
    • Statistical evidence supports that mean time spent on new landing page is greater than mean time spent on the old landing page.
  • Overall, 54 users converted to subscribers out of 100.
    • 33 users out of 50 converted to subscribers with the new landing page, while 21 out of 50 converted to subscribers for the old landing page.
    • Statistical evidence supports that conversion rate for the new landing page is greater than the old landing page.
  • Cannot reject the null that coversion to subscriber is independent of preferred languge.
    • For preferred language as English, 21 out of 32 converted to subscribers.
      • For the new landing page, 10 out of 16 converted.
    • For preferred language as French, 15 out of 34 converted to subscribers.
      • For the new landing page, 12 out of 17 converted.
    • For preferred language as Spanish, 18 out of 34 converted to subscribers.
      • For the new landing page, 11 out of 17 converted.
  • Connot reject the null that time spent on the new page is the same for all three preferred languages.
    • For preferred language as English, the mean time spent on the new landing page is 6.66 minutes.
    • For preferred language as French, the mean time spent on the new landing page is 6.19 minutes.
    • For preferred language as Spanish, the mean time spent on the new landing page is 5.84 minutes.
  • The median time spent on the landing page is around 4 minutes for users that were not converted to a subscriber while the median time spent on the landing page is around 6.5 minutes for users that were converted to a subscriber.

Recommendations¶

  • Time spent on the landing page is substantially more for users that were converted.
    • Since conversion is a primary goal considered in the study, increasing time spent on the landing page is likely to help conversion rates.
    • Time spent on the new landing page is considerably greater compared to the old landing page, and so is the conversion rate.
    • Any content or features that increase time spent on the landing page should be further studied to determine the most significant drivers of this this metric.
  • Conversion rate overall was 55%, but was 66% for the new landing page vs. 42% for the old landing page.
    • Again, differences in the new landing page should be studied further. Determining the most improved features of the new landing page can help create an updated landing page with even greater time spent and an even greater conversion rate.
  • Overall, for both landing pages, there is not evidence suggesting that conversion rates differ by language.
    • The overall rate was 65.6% for English, 44.1% for French, and 52.9% for Spanish.
    • Considering the conversion rates for the new page only, it was 62.5% for English, 70.6% for French, and 64.7% for Spanish.
    • By far the greatest improvement from the new landing page was seen by users preferring French. This improvement should be studied more to see what users that prefer French saw as the biggest improvement. These improvements may then be applied to further increase conversion rates for English and Spanish.
  • For the new landing page, time spent on the page was found to be unrelated to preferred language.
    • This suggests that the new landing pages for French and Spanish do a better job at indentifying features more relavent for those languages. They may also do a better job at simply translating into French and Spanish.
